## Audience And Promise

- Audience: ages 4-8, quiet-time viewing.
- Length target: 5-7 minutes.
- Feeling: sunny, playful, patient, encouraging.
- Moral: steady effort can beat careless speed.
- Discussion question: "What is something you can do slowly and carefully?"

## Narration Script

On the edge of a sunny meadow, where yellow flowers nodded in the breeze, there
lived a very fast hare.

The hare had long legs, quick feet, and ears that bounced when he ran. He loved
to race along the path, around the bushes, over the little hill, and back again.

"Look how fast I am!" he said.

The birds saw him flash past.

The squirrels saw him leap over stones.

The butterflies saw him zip through the meadow so quickly that the flowers
wobbled after he passed.

The hare was fast, and he knew it.

Near the same meadow lived a tortoise.

The tortoise moved slowly. Step by step, he crossed the grass. He did not rush.
He did not show off. When he wanted to reach a place, he simply kept going.

One morning, the hare saw the tortoise walking along the path.

"Good morning, Tortoise!" called the hare. "Are you walking to the old oak tree?"

"Yes," said the tortoise.

The hare laughed.

"At that speed, you may arrive next week!"

The tortoise looked up calmly.

"Slow steps can still reach the tree," he said.

The hare flicked his ears.

"Would you like to race me?"

The meadow grew quiet. The birds leaned forward on their branches. The
squirrels stopped nibbling. Even the butterflies hovered in place.

The tortoise thought for a moment.

"Yes," he said. "I will race you."

The finish line would be the old oak tree at the far side of the meadow.

The fox agreed to start the race.

"Ready," said the fox. "Set. Go!"

The hare sprang forward like a leaf in the wind. In just a few breaths, he was
far ahead.

The tortoise began walking.

Step.

Step.

Step.

The hare looked back. The tortoise was only a small shape on the path.

"This is too easy," said the hare. "I have plenty of time."

He found a soft patch of grass beneath a shady bush.

"I will rest for just a little while," he said.

He curled up, closed his eyes, and fell asleep.

The tortoise kept walking.

Step.

Step.

Step.

The sun moved slowly across the sky. The birds sang. The meadow breeze touched
the flowers. The tortoise passed the shady bush, but he did not stop to tease
the sleeping hare.

He kept going.

Step.

Step.

Step.

At last, the hare opened one eye. Then both eyes.

"Oh!" he cried. "The race!"

He jumped up and ran faster than ever. His ears streamed behind him. His paws
flew over the path.

But near the old oak tree, the tortoise was already taking his final steps.

Step.

Step.

Step.

The tortoise crossed the finish line.

The animals cheered.

The hare arrived a moment later, out of breath and very surprised.

The tortoise smiled kindly.

"You are very fast," he said. "But I kept going."

The hare lowered his ears.

"I thought speed was enough," he said. "I forgot to finish."

From that day on, the hare still loved to run. But he learned to respect slow,
steady work.

And the tortoise kept walking in his own patient way.

Their story reminds us:

Fast is helpful, but steady keeps its promise.
