## Audience And Promise

- Audience: ages 4-8, parent-approved bedtime or quiet-time viewing.
- Length target: 5-7 minutes.
- Feeling: warm, safe, kind, forest bedtime.
- Moral: kindness is never wasted, and even the smallest friend can help.
- Discussion question: "Can you remember a time when a small kindness helped
  someone?"

## Narration Script

In a warm green forest, where the leaves whispered softly and the sunlight
fell in golden patches, there lived a great lion.

The lion was strong. His paws were wide. His mane was bright and full. When he
walked through the forest, the birds grew quiet, the rabbits hid in the grass,
and even the monkeys watched from the highest branches.

But on this morning, the lion was not roaring. He was sleeping.

He had stretched out beneath a shady tree. His head rested on his paws, and his
tail gave one slow swish every now and then. He looked peaceful, like a very
large, very fuzzy hill.

Nearby, a tiny mouse was hurrying home.

The mouse had been gathering seeds. She carried one little seed in her paws and
was thinking about breakfast. She did not see the lion's nose. She did not see
his whiskers. She only saw a soft brown path in front of her.

So she ran straight across the lion's face.

The lion woke with a snort. His eyes opened wide. One great paw came down, and
the little mouse found herself gently but firmly trapped.

"Who has woken me?" rumbled the lion.

The mouse trembled. She was so small that the lion could barely feel her under
his paw.

"Please, great lion," she squeaked. "I did not mean to wake you. I was only
running home. If you let me go, I promise I will remember your kindness."

The lion blinked.

"You?" he said. "You are a tiny mouse. How could you ever help me?"

Then the lion laughed. It was not a cruel laugh. It was a surprised laugh, the
kind that shakes your shoulders when something seems too strange to believe.

The little mouse kept still.

The lion looked at her tiny paws, her bright eyes, and the seed she had dropped
beside her.

"Very well," said the lion at last. "Run home, little one. Be more careful next
time."

He lifted his paw.

The mouse bowed her head, picked up her seed, and ran into the grass. But she
did not forget.

A few days later, the forest was quiet again. The mouse was nibbling breakfast
near a patch of wildflowers when she heard a deep sound rolling through the
trees.

It was not thunder.

It was not wind.

It was the lion.

The mouse followed the sound. She slipped between roots, under leaves, and over
small stones until she reached a clearing.

There was the great lion. He was caught in a strong rope net. The more he
pulled, the tighter the ropes held him. His mane was tangled, his paws were
tired, and his voice was full of worry.

The mouse remembered the morning under the tree.

"Great lion," she called, "hold still. I can help."

The lion looked down and saw the tiny mouse.

He was too tired to laugh.

The mouse climbed onto the rope. She used her small sharp teeth to nibble and
nibble. One rope loosened. Then another. Then another.

It took time. The lion waited. The mouse kept working.

At last, the final rope snapped.

The lion stood up and shook his mane. He was free.

He lowered his great head until his nose was close to the mouse.

"Little friend," he said softly, "I was wrong. You helped me when I could not
help myself."

The mouse smiled.

"You were kind to me," she said. "I only kept my promise."

From that day on, the lion watched where he stepped, and the mouse walked
through the forest with a little more courage.

And everyone who heard their story remembered this:

A kindness may seem small when you give it, but one day it can become very big
indeed.

## Voice Direction

- Pace: slow and warm, about 130-145 words per minute.
- Tone: bedtime storyteller.
- Avoid: scary roar, sarcasm, dramatic villain voice.
- Character notes: lion is deep but kind; mouse is small and brave, not frantic.
