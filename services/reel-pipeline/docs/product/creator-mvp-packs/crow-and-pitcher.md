## Audience And Promise

- Audience: ages 4-8, quiet problem-solving story.
- Length target: 5-7 minutes.
- Feeling: warm afternoon, thoughtful, hopeful.
- Moral: patient thinking can solve hard problems.
- Discussion question: "What can you try when the first idea does not work?"

## Narration Script

One warm afternoon, when the sun shone high and the fields were quiet, a crow
flew across the countryside.

The crow had shiny black feathers, bright eyes, and strong wings. Usually, she
liked warm days. Warm days made the berries sweet and the sky clear.

But this day was very warm.

The crow had flown a long way. Her wings were tired. Her throat felt dry.

"I need a drink of water," she said.

She looked beside the path. No pond.

She looked near the fence. No stream.

She looked under the trees. No puddle.

The crow kept flying, but she did not give up.

At last, near an old garden wall, she saw something on the ground.

It was a tall clay pitcher.

The crow landed beside it and hopped closer.

"Maybe there is water inside," she said.

She looked into the pitcher. There was water, cool and clear, but it was far
down at the bottom.

The crow pushed her beak into the pitcher.

She stretched.

She leaned.

She stood on the tips of her claws.

But her beak could not reach the water.

The crow stepped back.

"That did not work," she said.

She tried to tip the pitcher over. She pushed with her shoulder. She pushed
with her wing. She pushed with both feet.

The pitcher was too heavy.

"That did not work either," said the crow.

She felt disappointed. She was still thirsty, and the water was right there.

But the crow did not flap away in anger. She stood quietly and thought.

Sometimes a problem looks too big when you only try one answer.

The crow walked around the pitcher. She looked at the narrow opening. She
looked at the water far below. She looked at the ground around her feet.

There were small pebbles everywhere.

The crow picked up one pebble in her beak and dropped it into the pitcher.

Plink.

The pebble fell into the water.

The crow looked inside.

The water had risen just a tiny bit.

Her eyes grew bright.

She picked up another pebble.

Plink.

Then another.

Plink.

Then another.

Plink.

Pebble by pebble, the water rose.

The crow grew tired, but she kept going. She did not need one giant answer. She
needed many small tries.

Plink.

Plink.

Plink.

At last, the water was high enough.

The crow dipped her beak into the pitcher and took a cool drink.

"Ah," she said. "That was worth thinking about."

She drank until she felt better. Then she spread her wings and flew to a shady
tree to rest.

The old pitcher stayed by the garden wall, with pebbles resting quietly at the
bottom.

And the crow's story reminds us:

When a problem is hard, stop, look closely, and try one small idea at a time.
